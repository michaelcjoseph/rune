## Hooks & openers

- [2026-06-05 · source: curiosity-gap] Open with a half-told story or unresolved claim that activates curiosity, but supply just enough context that the reader actually cares about the answer.
- [2026-06-05 · source: thread-tldr-first] Write the summary takeaway first as an outline, then open with a compressed hook that names the reader, the payoff, and your credibility before delivering single-sentence skimmable points.
- [2026-06-05 · source: withheld-payoff] Name the synthesis you are about to deliver but place the actual payoff just past a gating point, so anticipation of the promised conclusion pulls the reader forward.
- [2026-06-05 · source: withhold-the-concept] Withhold the central concept until after you have made the reader feel why it matters, so its definition lands as discovery rather than exposition.

## Argument & persuasion

- [2026-06-05 · source: ground-the-abstraction] Make an abstract dynamic persuasive by grounding it in a single observed behavior so specific it reads as evidence of the pattern rather than mere illustration.
- [2026-06-05 · source: fixed-template-proof] Prove a framework's universality by running every example through an identical fixed template, so the repeated shape itself becomes the argument.
- [2026-06-05 · source: personify-the-internal] Turn an invisible internal process into named characters with competing wants, so readers recognize themselves instead of arguing with the claim.
- [2026-06-05 · source: invert-the-default] Persuade by inverting the reader's default assumption so that clinging to the old view feels irrational, rather than merely arguing your position is correct.
- [2026-06-05 · source: define-by-elimination] Define a new concept by systematically eliminating the alternatives first, so your proposed answer arrives feeling inevitable rather than asserted.
- [2026-06-05 · source: contrast-to-clarify] Clarify abstract ideas through structured comparisons that isolate a single variable, teaching each concept by contrast against its opposite.
- [2026-06-05 · source: coin-a-name] Coin one vivid, memorable name for your core idea so it becomes a portable framework others can cite and repeat.

## Clarity, style & voice

- [2026-06-05 · source: spoken-register] Default to the register you would use explaining the idea aloud to a sharp friend; formal or ornate phrasing usually conceals a thin argument rather than elevating a strong one.
- [2026-06-05 · source: useful-and-true] Aim to tell readers something that matters and that they did not already know, stated as boldly as you can without becoming false.
- [2026-06-05 · source: novelty-times-resonance] Earn virality by pairing a genuinely novel angle with emotional resonance through concrete stories, specific examples, and authentic voice rather than recycled generic advice.
- [2026-06-05 · source: perspective-over-information] Anchor nonfiction in instrumentally useful, quantifiable guidance while threading a distinct opinionated voice and personal stakes through it, since readers subscribe to perspective as much as information.

## Structure & revision

- [2026-06-05 · source: outline-the-argument] Spend more time outlining than drafting — fix a single arguable thesis and a header sequence that reads as a coherent logical path before writing prose, to prevent a midway collapse.
- [2026-06-05 · source: cut-what-doesnt-earn-it] Treat every word as costly to the reader and free to you, so delete any sentence, section, or whole draft that does not earn its place once structure is settled.
- [2026-06-05 · source: defamiliarize-before-editing] Let a draft rest and change your context before revising — switch editors, print it, or wait a week — so you read your own work as a stranger would and trust only the criticism several readers independently repeat.

## Drafting process & distribution

- [2026-06-05 · source: draft-to-discover] Draft fast and badly with placeholders for gaps, treating writing as thinking rather than transcription so the strongest ideas surface during composition rather than before it.
- [2026-06-05 · source: cadence-plus-flagship] A predictable publishing rhythm builds retention, but occasional outsized effort on a deeply-researched flagship piece accounts for a disproportionate share of new-audience growth — budget effort asymmetrically.
